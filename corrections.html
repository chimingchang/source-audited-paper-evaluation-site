Submission checklist

What A Useful Challenge Should Include

Paper and claim

Name the paper id, affected claim, and whether the issue concerns paper-level scoring or a registered claim split.

Public source

Provide an arXiv id, paper title, theorem, section, figure, no-go result, review, or other checkable public source.

Proposed effect

State whether the evidence should change score, maturity, cap, claim fate, uncertainty band, source miss risk, or audit tags.

Boundary status

Flag whether the issue touches withheld-signal rows. Withheld provenance can motivate an audit, but visible public evidence must carry the public correction.

Lifecycle

How Corrections Become Ranking Changes

1

Evidence intake

Record the affected paper, claim, public source, and proposed scoring surface.

2

Audit update

Update source audit, evidence pack, claim-fate note, or robustness audit before changing rank.

3

Scoring rerun

Regenerate scoring reports only after the audit layer explains the correction.

4

Versioned release

Ranking changes enter the next candidate or frozen release, not by silent in-place edits.

5

Public gate

The redacted public site is rebuilt and deployed only after the publication-readiness gate passes.

Evidence boundaries

What Does Not Count By Itself

  • Authority signals. Author status, institution, venue, or reputation cannot replace source-audited evidence.
  • Citation momentum. Citation count may identify a trail to inspect, but it is not itself a score argument.
  • Unscoped disagreement. A challenge should name the affected claim or scoring surface, not only a global preference.
  • Withheld provenance. Withheld calibration details are not public evidence. They can explain why a public row has an audit tag, but public score changes must be justified by visible sources or versioned audit fields.
